The UAE accumulated this arsenal over the course of several decades, with an eye on the threat from Iran. Forty years before these Iranian drones and missiles rained down on its major cities, another attack undoubtedly influenced the Emirates' procurement decisions.

On Nov. 25, 1986, during the Iran-Iraq War, air-launched missiles hit two Emirati oil platforms 100 miles from Abu Dhabi. While it did not say so publicly, the UAE government had no doubt that Tehran had mounted the attack, investigative journalists Jack Anderson and Dale Van Atta reported at the time. In those days, the Emirati air force and air defense were much more modest than today, outmatched in quality and quantity by Iran’s air and naval forces built in the preceding decade by the last shah.

Abu Dhabi requested shoulder-fired FIM-92 Stinger missiles from the United States to bolster air defenses around critical assets and infrastructure. When the Reagan administration refused, it turned to the Soviet Union for equivalent systems, an early demonstration of its willingness to shop around to ensure its defense requirements were met.

The Iran-Iraq War was followed by the 1991 Persian Gulf War, when a U.S.-led military coalition ejected Saddam Hussein’s Iraq from Kuwait, which Hussein had annexed the year before. The UAE pursued a military buildup in the 1990s, ordering AH-64 Apache attack helicopters and F-16 Vipers. The F-16 order was unprecedented, as Abu Dhabi secured a deal for 80 custom-made variants more advanced than any flown by the U.S. Air Force, the Block 60 “Desert Falcon." For good measure, the UAE simultaneously ordered additional Dassault Mirage 2000 fighters from France. The Emirates will replace the Mirage 2000 fleet with 80 advanced Dassault Rafale F4 fighters ordered in a landmark December 2021 deal.

In the 2000s, the UAE began upgrading its ground-based air defenses. It first ordered medium-range Pantsir-S1 systems from Russia, specialized for providing point defense for important targets. In the 2010s, it acquired the Patriot PAC-3 and the THAAD, giving it arguably the most advanced regional air defense outside of Israel.

After normalizing ties with Israel in 2020 through the Abraham Accords, Abu Dhabi also discreetly acquired Israeli-made Barak and SPYDER systems. As if that didn’t diversify its arsenal enough, it has also ordered medium-range KM-SAM systems from South Korea.

Together, these systems provide a formidable multi-layered air defense against a range of threats, from ballistic missiles to low-flying drones. It’s now facing its greatest combat test yet.
